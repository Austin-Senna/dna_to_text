
Pooling long genomic and protein embeddings into one sequence representation
Executive assessment
Your teammate’s objection is directionally right, but the sharpest version is not that “chunks do not have meaning.” The real issue is that arbitrary chunking plus symmetric pooling turns a structured biological sequence into a bag of averaged windows, and that choice bakes in strong assumptions about how signal is distributed. Mean-over-tokens assumes evidence is spread across residues within a chunk; mean-over-chunks assumes evidence is spread across chunk positions and that chunks are exchangeable. Those assumptions are often reasonable for broad compositional or distributed regulatory signals, but they are often wrong for long CDS or proteins whose labels depend on sparse motifs, a few critical residues, terminal regions, or domain architecture. The strongest empirical DNA benchmark available shows that for frozen zero-shot DNA embeddings, mean token pooling beats summary-token and max pooling very consistently across five genomic foundation models and 52 binary classification datasets. But that result mostly answers the question “how should I pool a single model window?” It does not settle the harder hierarchical problem you care about: how to pool a long CDS after chunking. 

Across the literature, the most defensible summary is this: mean pooling is the best general frozen-embedding default when you need a robust, low-variance, model-agnostic baseline, especially for DNA sequence classification benchmarks. But mean pooling is also the easiest way to erase sparse local signal and chunk heterogeneity, which is exactly the failure mode your teammate is worried about. Protein-language-model papers that study pooling more directly repeatedly show that once tasks depend on sparse functional residues, local substructures, or long proteins, learned or locality-aware pooling can outperform mean, max, and CLS/BOS pooling. In contrast, a broad protein transfer-learning study found that mean pooling still wins on average across many datasets, while max/iDCT can occasionally win on more homogeneous mutation-effect datasets. So the literature does not support “mean is always right”; it supports “mean is a strong baseline, but not the last word when biological signal is sparse or region-specific.” 

For long CDS/protein family classification under a fixed compute budget, the most important point is that your current baseline only tests one biological hypothesis: distributed signal everywhere. It does not test the alternatives that matter most for long coding sequences: sparse within-chunk motifs, sparse across-chunk domain relevance, terminal asymmetry, and domain-architecture effects. For that reason, the first sprint should not jump straight to a fully learned global attention head. Instead, it should first run a small but diagnostic fixed-pooling slate that can falsify specific assumptions. My recommendation is: keep the current mean→mean baseline, add B, add D, and add F before learned attention; treat C as a diagnostic rather than a likely production winner. If you must choose only among B/C/D, prioritize D, then B, then C. Learned attention should come next, not first, because you said you care especially about frozen embeddings and shallow probes—and learned attention muddies that interpretation. 

What the literature actually shows
Original DNA and protein foundation-model papers often adopt a single convenient sequence summary rather than rigorously comparing pooling operators. DNABERT follows BERT-style sequence modeling with a [CLS] token intended to represent the whole sequence during fine-tuning, and the model is framed around standard sequence-classification practice. DNABERT-2’s public usage examples explicitly show mean and max pooling over token embeddings, and in the GUE+ long-sequence setting, DNABERT baselines handle inputs beyond the 512-token limit by splitting long inputs into 512-bp pieces and averaging their embeddings before the output layer. That is directly relevant to your use case: hierarchical averaging is already used in the literature, but it is adopted mainly as an engineering workaround, not because chunk averaging was shown to be biologically optimal. 

The original Nucleotide Transformer paper emphasizes probing frozen embeddings with logistic regression or small MLP probes across layers, and it computes mean embeddings over the sequence locations corresponding to a genomic element for t-SNE and probing analyses. That paper is very informative about frozen-representation quality, layer choice, and probing methodology, but it does not provide a direct CLS-versus-mean-versus-max sequence-pooling benchmark comparable to the later DNA foundation-model benchmark. In other words, for Nucleotide Transformer, the original paper is primarily evidence about frozen representation content, not about the superiority of one pooling rule. 

HyenaDNA is more interesting for your question because it explicitly changes pooling by context length. In its chromatin-profile experiments, the 1k model used average pooling across all tokens, whereas the longer-context 8k model performed better using the last token as the decoder input. In its ultra-long species-classification setup, HyenaDNA again used token averaging for shorter 1k/32k models but switched to last-token classification for 250k–1M contexts. And in its frozen biotype-embedding experiment, when the input exceeded the model context window, sequences were chunked and embeddings averaged over chunks. Taken together, HyenaDNA is evidence that pooling is not architecture invariant and not scale invariant: the best summarizer can change with context length and task. 

Caduceus and GROVER also show that the field is not standardized. Caduceus’s public genomics-benchmark setup uses a mean-pooling plus linear projection decoder head for downstream fine-tuning in the benchmark configuration. GROVER, by contrast, analyzes the [CLS] token as a window summary for 510-token windows and also studies average trained embeddings of genomic regions relative to functional annotations. So even among high-profile genomic models, one finds mean-pooled fine-tuning heads, summary-token analyses, and window-level regional averages, rather than one agreed best practice. 

The strongest direct DNA evidence now comes from the 2025 five-model benchmark spanning DNABERT-2, Nucleotide Transformer V2, HyenaDNA, Caduceus-Ph, and GROVER. That study compared summary token, mean token, and max token pooling for 52 binary sequence-classification datasets and found that mean token pooling significantly outperformed both summary-token and maximum pooling for the large majority of datasets for every model. The reported average AUC gains from switching summary-token pooling to mean-token pooling were 4.0% for DNABERT-2, 6.8% for NT-v2, 8.7% for HyenaDNA, 5.9% for Caduceus-Ph, and 1.4% for GROVER, with DeLong testing at p < 0.01. That is the cleanest answer in genomics today for single-window frozen embedding evaluation. 

Protein-language-model work is richer on pooling. A broad transfer-learning study across ESM-2, ESM C, and AMPLIFY found that mean embeddings consistently outperformed other simple compression methods on average, including max pooling and BOS/CLS-style summaries, although max pooling and iDCT occasionally beat mean pooling on some DMS datasets. At the same time, more targeted studies on protein tasks show that learned attention, locality-aware pooling, optimal-transport pooling, and attention-derived weighting can outperform mean/CLS/max once the task depends on sparse local determinants or long proteins. That split is important for your application: the mean-pooling literature is strongest when asking for a robust default across many tasks, while the learned/locality-aware literature is strongest when asking how not to erase sparse biological signal. 

Pooling strategies and the biological assumptions they impose
The table below is a synthesis of the pooling literature and the inductive biases each choice imposes. The key point is that pooling is not just compression; it is a biological hypothesis about where the label lives. That is why the same backbone can look “good” or “bad” depending on the summarizer.

Two practical consequences follow for long CDS. First, within-chunk pooling and across-chunk pooling solve different problems. A method like B only addresses within-chunk dilution; it still averages informative and non-informative chunks together. If your worry is genuinely “heterogeneous sequence regions,” F is the more direct test. Second, chunk boundaries are part of the model once motifs can be split across windows. For coding DNA, chunking should therefore be done in a way that respects the CDS rather than treating the sequence like arbitrary text.

What to test first for long CDS and protein family classification
For your exact use case, the literature is strongest on token pooling inside a single model window and much weaker on hierarchical pooling over arbitrarily chunked long CDS. That means your sprint should be designed to answer the missing question directly rather than assuming the DNA benchmark settles it. The correct experimental target is not “which pooling is universally best,” but “which latent biological picture best matches my task?” 

A minimal, cheap, and interpretable sprint is:

Priority	Variant	What it tests	Why it matters
Keep	Baseline: mean over tokens, then mean over chunks	Fully distributed signal	Existing reference point
High	B: max over tokens, then mean over chunks	Sparse signal within chunks	Tests whether local motifs/residues are being washed out inside windows
High	D: concat[first chunk pooled, last chunk pooled, global mean]	Terminal asymmetry + global distributed signal	Best fixed-pooling test of “different sequence regions play different roles”
High	F: token pooling, then max over chunks	Sparse signal across chunks	Most direct response to the “heterogeneous chunks” criticism
Medium	C: first chunk only	Strong 5′/N-terminal dominance	Useful diagnostic; unlikely best generic production method
Medium	G: concat[first, last, global mean, global max]	Combined terminal + distributed + sparse global signal	Strong second-round fixed method if D or F helps
Later	E: learned attention over chunk vectors or token embeddings	Task-specific region weighting	Add only after fixed pooling tells you simple averaging is the bottleneck
Opportunistic	H: domain/motif-aware pooling	Biology-informed windows	High upside if annotations are reliable

If you must choose among only B/C/D for the first sprint, I would rank them D > B > C. D is the best low-cost test of region heterogeneity, B is the best low-cost test of within-window motif dilution, and C is mostly a falsification probe. But I would strongly add F even if that means dropping A, because A and the whole CLS/first-token family already look weak in frozen DNA benchmarking, whereas F directly tests the part of your teammate’s criticism that B does not address. 

The experiment also needs a few design controls that are easy to overlook. Chunk the CDS in a coding-aware way so that chunk starts are consistent and not arbitrary with respect to the sequence. Keep chunk size and overlap fixed across all pooling conditions. Do not change the backbone or the layer while comparing pooling. Cache the same encoder outputs and only vary pooling, otherwise you are no longer measuring pooling. And because chunk boundaries can themselves create artifacts, include a boundary-sensitivity check: rerun the best fixed pooling method under a shifted chunk offset on a small validation slice. If the ranking flips, chunk placement rather than embedding quality may be driving the result. Those are not details; they are part of the hypothesis test. 

For metrics, report macro-F1, MCC, balanced accuracy, and if the problem is binary or one-vs-rest, AUROC and AUPRC. For retrieval-style evaluation, report Recall@k, MRR/MAP, and nDCG. For long CDS/protein-family settings specifically, stratify results by sequence length and, if available, by number of annotated domains or another proxy for architectural heterogeneity. The most useful plots are not just global scores; they are performance-versus-length and performance-versus-domain-count. That is exactly where mean pooling should fail if the teammate’s criticism is correct. 

For uncertainty, use paired bootstrap confidence intervals over the same held-out examples for all pooling variants. Where AUROC is central, also run DeLong’s test between the strongest alternatives, mirroring the DNA benchmark. For macro-F1 and retrieval metrics, use a paired permutation or bootstrap test. Report seed-averaged performance, but do not rely on seed variance alone; pooling methods often differ more by which sequences they help than by optimizer randomness. 

The expected interpretations are straightforward and useful. If B > baseline but F ≈ baseline, then you mainly have within-chunk dilution. If F > baseline and especially if D also helps, then the problem is cross-chunk heterogeneity and exchangeability assumptions are wrong. If C is surprisingly strong, the label may be dominated by early-region cues, but you should treat that as something to verify, not trust automatically. If G > D, then your task likely needs both distributed and sparse global evidence. And if E later beats every fixed pooling method while the encoder stays frozen, the right interpretation is not “the encoder was bad”; it is “the signal was there, but fixed pooling hid it.” That is exactly the reason to delay learned attention until after the fixed-pooling slate. 

Final recommendation
For the question you actually care about—does the frozen representation already contain useful family signal, and is mean→mean hiding it?—I would not include learned attention pooling from day one in the main benchmark. The evidence base says that learned or locality-aware pooling can indeed help when biological signal is sparse or region-specific, but those methods also introduce head capacity and weaken the interpretability of a frozen-embedding comparison. The clean first sprint is therefore:

Keep the current mean→mean baseline.
Add B.
Add D.
Add F.
Add C as a diagnostic only.
If you insist on limiting phase one to the user-proposed B/C/D set, run D first, then B, then C. After that, if either D or F gives a real and stable gain, add G. Only then move to E, preferably as a lightweight attention-over-chunk-vectors head rather than full attention over all concatenated tokens, because it is much cheaper and isolates whether chunk weighting—not token-level sequence modeling—is the missing ingredient. 

The bottom line is this. The literature strongly supports mean pooling as a default single-window probe, especially in frozen DNA embedding benchmarks. But for long CDS after chunking, the literature does not justify stopping at mean→mean. In your setting, the most relevant risk is not that chunks are meaningless; it is that arbitrary chunks are treated as equally meaningful. Test that assumption directly with D and F before you pay the interpretability cost of learned attention. If they fail, your teammate’s critique is probably overstated. If they win, you have strong evidence that the bottleneck is pooling, not necessarily the encoder. 

Open questions and limitations
The main limitation in the literature is that very few papers directly study hierarchical pooling over arbitrarily chunked long CDS. Most genomic papers compare token pooling within one model window, or they chunk long inputs and average as a practical workaround without turning chunk aggregation itself into the experimental variable. Protein pooling papers are therefore more informative than genomic papers about what happens when the relevant signal is sparse, local, or architecture-dependent. 

I also did not find strong prior literature directly validating first+last+global concatenation as a standard genomic sequence-pooling recipe. That idea is best treated as a well-motivated experiment, not as a literature-established norm. Similarly, I found much stronger evidence for learned/locality-aware pooling in proteins than for DNA foundation models. So if your downstream target is a CDS/protein-family problem, the right reading of the literature is: borrow protein pooling ideas, but validate them carefully on your own chunked CDS setup rather than assuming transfer.
